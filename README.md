## Key Insight Examples

These insights reflect recurring patterns observed across ESG disclosures, infrastructure projects, and climate strategies.

### 1. Scope 3 Blind Spot

Most firms disclose Scope 1 emissions but omit Scope 3 exposure, despite supply chains driving the majority of climate risk.

→ Creates a false signal of performance
→ Leads to underestimation of transition risk
→ Weakens decision-making for investors and regulators

---

### 2. Reporting vs. Value Disconnect

Many companies focus on sustainability reporting but fail to link disclosures to sustainability management accounting.

→ ESG data is presented without connection to financial performance
→ Missed opportunity to show cost savings (e.g., energy efficiency, resource optimization)
→ Weak integration into strategy, operations, and decision-making

In practice, this means:

* Energy initiatives are reported, but cost reductions are not quantified
* Productivity gains from sustainability investments are not measured
* Margin improvements linked to resource efficiency are rarely disclosed

→ Sustainability remains a communication exercise rather than a value driver

---

### 3. Renewable Energy Misrepresentation

Renewable energy claims are often reported without location-based or grid context.

→ Masks dependency on carbon-intensive grids
→ Overstates actual decarbonization progress
→ Distorts infrastructure and policy decisions

---

### 4. Selective Disclosure Strategy

Firms tend to report metrics that are easy to measure and favorable, while omitting high-risk or uncertain areas.

→ Creates impression management rather than full transparency
→ Limits comparability across firms
→ Reduces reliability of ESG data for decision-making
