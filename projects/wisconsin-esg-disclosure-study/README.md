# Energy & Climate Disclosure — Wisconsin Firms

This study evaluates the credibility and completeness of ESG disclosures across 20 Wisconsin-based companies, focusing on energy use, emissions reporting, and alignment with climate transition goals.

  Full paper: :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

## What this project does

- Assesses corporate disclosures using GRI 302 (Energy) and GRI 305 (Emissions)
- Evaluates Scope 1–3 reporting completeness and credibility
- Examines third-party assurance and transparency gaps
- Identifies patterns of selective disclosure and impression management

## Key Findings

- Firms report basic metrics (energy use, Scope 1) but omit critical areas (Scope 3, renewable sourcing)
- Third-party verification is inconsistent → weakens credibility
- Larger firms do NOT necessarily disclose better data
- ESG reporting is often strategic (legitimacy-driven), not fully accountable

## Why it matters

Corporate disclosures are central to climate accountability, but current practices create a gap between reported commitments and actual performance.

This project highlights:
- Risks in relying on ESG reports for decision-making  
- Need for stronger standards (TCFD, ISSB)  
- Misalignment with Wisconsin’s clean energy transition goals  

## Methods

- Content analysis of 20 firms (2022–2024)
- GRI-based indicator scoring (energy + emissions)
- Comparative sector analysis
- Spearman correlation (revenue vs disclosure quality)

## Output

- Academic publication  
- ESG disclosure scoring framework  
- Policy-relevant insights for climate governance
